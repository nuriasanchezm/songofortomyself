about

Have you ever encountered a poem, song, or quote, that resonated profoundly with you, only to find out later on that it is a forgery, and that the original version is actually terrible? That happened to me as an undergraduate, when I first read León Felipe's Spanish translation (which he called, actually, paráfrasis) of Song of Myself by Walt Whitman (United States of America, 1819-92), in a hardcover book as heavy as my heart at that moment. The last stanza of the last part stroke me as the most romantic literary piece I had ever read. But when I looked it up on the Internet to learn more about it, I found two things: another Spanish translation that butchered both the form and meaning I was fascinated by, and Whitman's original... which was, sadly, exactly as bad.

In this short project I aim to reconcile the three versions to determine what is gained and what is lost in the passage between English and Spanish, and I will provide further proof on why León Felipe's version excels, based on Jorge Luis Borges's critique of the text (his translation can be found online, but it is not complete).
